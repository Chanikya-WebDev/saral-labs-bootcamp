    Electromagnetic Waves

    Technical Deep-Dive ~30 min read 5-question quiz

    § 1 The Inconsistency in Ampère's Circuital Law

    In classical magnetostatics, Ampère's Circuital Law relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the net conduction current passing through any surface bounded by that loop. Before Maxwell's intervention, this law was stated as:

    Ampère's Law (Original Form)
    \[\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 \, I_{\text{enclosed}}\]
    ⚠️
    The flaw: This law was derived under the assumption of steady currents only. For time-varying fields, it produces mathematically inconsistent results — as the capacitor paradox below demonstrates.

    The Charging Capacitor Paradox

    Consider a parallel-plate capacitor being charged by a time-dependent conduction current \( I(t) \). Apply Ampère's Law to a circular Amperian loop \( C \) centred on the wire, but choose two different surfaces both bounded by the same loop:

    Assumption 1The wire is thin and the conduction current \(I(t)\) varies slowly enough that the quasi-static approximation holds, yet fast enough to observe charging effects.
    Assumption 2The medium between the plates is vacuum (\(\kappa = 1\)), so fringe fields at the plate edges are negligible.
    • Surface \(S_1\): A flat disk spanning the loop. The wire pierces this surface, so the enclosed current is \(I_{\text{enclosed}} = I(t)\). By Ampère's Law:
    \[\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 \, I(t)\]
    • Surface \(S_2\): A balloon-shaped surface that bulges out and passes between the capacitor plates. No conduction current pierces this surface (\(I = 0\)):
    \[\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0\]
    🔑
    The contradiction: The line integral of \(\mathbf{B}\) around a fixed loop must be unique — independent of which surface you choose to cap the loop. Getting \(\mu_0 I(t)\) vs. \(0\) for the same loop is a mathematical impossibility. This proves the original law is incomplete.

    § 2 Deriving the Displacement Current

    Maxwell resolved the inconsistency by invoking Gauss's Law and the principle of charge conservation. Let \(Q(t)\) be the charge on the capacitor plates and \(A\) be the plate area.

    Assumption 3The electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) is uniform between the plates and zero outside (neglecting edge effects). This is the parallel-plate approximation.

    Gauss's Law gives the electric flux through \(S_2\):

    Gauss's Law — Electric Flux
    \[\Phi_E = \int_{S_2} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q(t)}{\epsilon_0}\]

    Differentiate with respect to time to find the rate of change of flux:

    \[\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \frac{dQ}{dt} = \frac{I_c}{\epsilon_0}\]

    Because \(\frac{dQ}{dt} = I_c\) (the conduction current charging the plates), we can rearrange this as:

    Definition — Displacement Current
    \[I_d = \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}\]
    📌
    Physical meaning: \(I_d\) is not the flow of physical charges. It is the rate of change of electric flux — a purely field-theoretic quantity. Between the capacitor plates, \(I_d = I_c\) exactly, so "current" is conserved across any surface.

    Differential Form — Displacement Current Density

    In differential (point-by-point) form, the displacement current density is:

    Displacement Current Density
    \[\mathbf{J}_d = \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\]

    This emerges from the requirement that taking the divergence of the modified Ampère equation must be consistent with the continuity equation \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0\).

    🔍
    Divergence consistency check: Taking \(\nabla \cdot\) of \(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}\), the left side is identically zero (divergence of curl). So \(\mu_0(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}) = 0\), implying \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0\). But continuity says \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}\), which is only zero for steady currents. Adding \(\mathbf{J}_d = \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\) fixes this exactly, since Gauss's Law gives \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0\).

    § 3 The Ampère-Maxwell Law

    Adding the displacement current to the original Ampère Law gives the Ampère-Maxwell Law — the fourth of Maxwell's equations:

    Ampère-Maxwell Law (Integral Form)
    \[\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 (I_c + I_d) = \mu_0 I_c + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}\]
    Ampère-Maxwell Law (Differential Form)
    \[\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}_c + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\]

    § 4 Maxwell's Four Equations — Complete Set

    Together, these four equations govern all classical electromagnetic phenomena. In a vacuum with no free charges or currents (\(\rho = 0, \, \mathbf{J} = 0\)), they become the foundation for deriving electromagnetic waves:

    Maxwell I — Gauss's Law
    Electric Flux
    \[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}\]
    Electric field lines originate on positive charges and terminate on negative charges. In vacuum: \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0\).
    Maxwell II — Gauss's Law for Magnetism
    No Magnetic Monopoles
    \[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\]
    Magnetic field lines always form closed loops. There are no isolated magnetic charges (monopoles) in nature.
    Maxwell III — Faraday's Law
    Electromagnetic Induction
    \[\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\]
    A time-varying magnetic field induces a circulating electric field. This is the principle behind generators and transformers.
    Maxwell IV — Ampère-Maxwell Law
    Magnetic Circulation
    \[\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\]
    A time-varying electric field induces a circulating magnetic field — Maxwell's key addition, enabling wave propagation in vacuum.

    § 5 Derivation of the EM Wave Equation

    Starting from Maxwell's equations in a source-free vacuum (\(\rho = 0, \mathbf{J} = 0\)), we derive the wave equation for the electric field. The derivation uses the curl of the curl technique:

    Take the curl of Faraday's Law (Maxwell III)

    Apply \(\nabla \times\) to both sides:

    \[\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})\]
    Apply the vector identity

    Use \(\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{A}\) on the left side:

    \[\nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})\]
    Substitute Maxwell I and Maxwell IV

    Since \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0\) (no free charges), and \(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\):

    \[0 - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = -\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}\]
    Arrive at the 3D Wave Equation

    Rearranging gives the final electromagnetic wave equation:

    \[\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}\]

    Comparing with the standard wave equation \(\nabla^2 f = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2}\), the phase velocity is:

    Speed of Light from First Principles
    \[c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}} \approx 3 \times 10^8 \; \text{m/s}\]
    ⚡
    Maxwell's greatest insight: This speed exactly matched the experimentally measured speed of light. Maxwell immediately concluded that light itself is an electromagnetic wave — unifying optics with electromagnetism in a single stroke.

    § 6 Plane Wave Solutions and Field Orthogonality

    For a monochromatic plane wave propagating in the \(+x\) direction, the electric field is polarized along \(\hat{j}\):

    Electric Field of a Plane Wave
    \[\mathbf{E}(x,t) = E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t)\,\hat{j}\]
    Assumption 4The wave is a transverse electromagnetic (TEM) wave. Both \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) are perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

    Applying Faraday's Law (\(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t\)) to find the corresponding magnetic field — the curl of \(\mathbf{E}\) for this geometry reduces to:

    \[\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = \frac{\partial E_y}{\partial x}\hat{k} = k E_0 \cos(kx - \omega t)\,\hat{k}\]

    Integrating \(-\partial B_z/\partial t = k E_0 \cos(kx-\omega t)\) with respect to time:

    Magnetic Field of a Plane Wave
    \[\mathbf{B}(x,t) = \frac{k}{\omega} E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t)\,\hat{k} = \frac{E_0}{c}\sin(kx-\omega t)\,\hat{k}\]

    Since the wave speed \(c = \omega/k\), we get \(B_0 = E_0/c\). This yields the fundamental ratio:

    Amplitude Ratio
    \[\frac{E_0}{B_0} = c = 3 \times 10^8 \;\text{m/s}\]
    📌
    Orthogonality: \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) are in phase, perpendicular to each other, and perpendicular to the propagation direction \(\hat{x}\). The energy is shared equally between both fields despite the magnitude difference by factor \(c\) (an SI units artefact).

    § 7 The Electromagnetic Spectrum

    All electromagnetic waves obey the same wave equation and travel at \(c\) in vacuum. The only parameter distinguishing different types is the frequency \(f\) (or equivalently, the wavelength \(\lambda = c/f\) or angular wavenumber \(k = 2\pi/\lambda\)).

    Dispersion Relation (Vacuum)
    \[\omega = ck \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad f = \frac{c}{\lambda}\]
    Band Frequency Wavelength Key Application
    Radio < 300 MHz > 1 m AM/FM broadcast, radar
    Microwave 300 MHz – 300 GHz 1 mm – 1 m WiFi, 5G, ovens, satellite
    Infrared 300 GHz – 430 THz 700 nm – 1 mm Thermal imaging, remote controls
    Visible Light 430 – 750 THz 400 – 700 nm Human vision, optical fibre
    Ultraviolet 750 THz – 30 PHz 10 – 400 nm Sterilisation, fluorescence
    X-ray 30 PHz – 30 EHz 0.01 – 10 nm Medical imaging, crystallography
    Gamma Ray > 30 EHz < 0.01 nm Cancer treatment, nuclear physics
    ⚠️
    Common misconception: The boundaries between bands are conventional, not physical. There is no sharp "edge" where radio waves end and microwaves begin — the spectrum is continuous. The classification exists purely for engineering and application purposes.
